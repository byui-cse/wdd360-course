# Instructor Preparation

---

## Course Outline

This course attempts to simulate working on a real project in a real team. The first 9 weeks will be spent adding features and fixing bugs on a project called SleepOutside, a simple outdoor gear retail site.

### Team Activities

During most weeks the students will have a required team activity that they should complete in a peer programming style. You can decide whether to form the groups yourself, or allow the students to self select. I recommend 3-4 students per group. Most of the points for these activities come from participation...only about 10% or so is for completion.

Make sure to stress to them that peer programming means that one student drives...they are on the editor typing code. The rest of the team tells them what to type. Most of the input should come from those NOT typing.

The team activities are used to make major changes to the application. The students will also be given a Trello board full of cards that fix bugs and add smaller bits of functionality. Teams should have the goal of completing at least one card per person in the team per week. Students should attempt the cards individually, but should feel welcome to reach out to their teammates for help as needed.

A typical week should have the following:

1. Reading assignment: 1-2hrs
2. Team activity: ~2hrs (If face-to-face 1hr of this will be classtime)
3. Trello Cards: ~3hrs
4. Professor led instruction: ~1-1.5hrs (either scheduled class or a weekly recorded office hour if online)

### Final Project

Part two is weeks 10-14 and will be spent building a new application that the teams have planned earlier in the course. There will still be readings, but no more team activities.

 This application should consist of both the frontend and backend with connected database.

## Instructor Time Expectations

The course is designed to minimize the amount of time you spend grading to allow for more time teaching and giving individualized attention to students that need it. Each week the students are required to turn in either notes or worked exercises. You should review those with the goal of extracting questions to address with the class. Encourage your students to include their questions in their notes!

They will also either arrange a meeting once a week between you and the team for a SCRUM style standup meeting, or you can have them submit that weekly report of status in Ilearn. These reports are your best tool for finding students whom you may need to reach out to. I would grade both of these activities on completion. Feel free to dock a few points on the notes if you feel they are putting in minimal effort.

If you can make it work the synchronous meeting report is WAY more effective than the Ilearn one. I highly recommend it if you can make it work. It would involve a 10-15 minute appointment for each group each week.

You should plan on holding an office hour each week. Record this for those that can't make it. Use this time to answer any questions you find in the notes or that the students attending bring, and then work on a coding example illustrating skills from the readings. Each week in the Teaching Notes I'll give an example project...use mine or feel free to come up with your own.

You may also consider preparing a short video introduction for each week to help the students plan their time.

## Final Grades

The grading for the course is a bit different. 30% of the grade will be made up of the weekly notes and team actities. The other 70% will be based off of a self-evaluation that the student performs. They will assess themselves on how well they have achieved the outcomes of the course, prepare a short video with their justifications, and then provide a portfolio of work to back up their claims. You should review their marks and their submitted work and adjust as necessary. A major part of that portfolio should be their final team project.
